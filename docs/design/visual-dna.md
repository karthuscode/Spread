# Purpose

Visual DNA defines the non-negotiable traits shared by every character in the *Spread* universe. A character should belong to the project before costume, colour, profession, or context is understood. Every Character Bible, Image Specification, concept study, and production asset inherits these rules.

Consistency is more important than isolated creativity. A brilliant character from another visual universe weakens the cast. Individual invention must happen inside a recognizable family language of caricature, asymmetry, material decay, and psychological expression.

Visual DNA creates family resemblance without making characters identical. Repetition of exact features is discouraged; repetition of artistic logic is required.

# Design Philosophy

*Spread* is a stylized psychological dark comedy populated by absurd, expressive mascots. Inner strangeness is visible: posture reveals obsession, proportions reveal temperament, and facial distortion reveals an unusual relationship with reality.

Grotesque caricature enlarges, compresses, bends, or misplaces features to reveal personality. It never means gore, bodily damage, or monster design. Characters remain human-readable, but believable anatomy is not a goal. The player should smile before feeling uncomfortable.

The governing priorities are:

- iconic before realistic;
- personality before profession;
- silhouette before detail;
- exaggeration before anatomy;
- expression before accuracy;
- memorable before believable;
- stylization before realism;
- psychological weirdness before horror;
- dark humour before violence.

No character should resemble a normal person in a themed costume. The entire construction must express this strange universe.

# Universal Character Rules

Every character must follow these rules:

- Use a large, expressive head relative to the torso.
- Push the neck longer, thinner, shorter, hidden, or otherwise less natural than expected.
- Select at least one body feature for deliberate, unmistakable exaggeration.
- Build slight or pronounced asymmetry into the face, shoulders, costume, or stance.
- Avoid perfectly clean, new, evenly fitted clothing.
- Avoid perfectly upright or balanced posture.
- Give the face one memorable feature that survives simplification.
- Construct a silhouette recognizable without interior detail.
- Make psychological instability more visible than physical danger.
- Let profession appear through secondary cues; personality must dominate the first read.
- Keep the character charismatic enough to invite attention.
- Preserve human readability without correcting forms toward realistic anatomy.

Exaggeration must feel intentional. Each distortion supports personality, hierarchy, or recognition. Strange does not mean visually confused: large shapes need clear relationships before accessories are inspected.

# Shape Language

Characters use large, simple masses balanced between rounded and angular forms. Curves may suggest softness, instability, vanity, or comic vulnerability. Wedges may suggest nervousness, control, rigidity, or sharp intelligence. Most designs combine both.

Discourage bilateral balance. Shift masses off-axis, vary shoulders, distort limb rhythms, or offset hair and costume. Use unexpected proportion changes: a huge head above a pinched torso or a narrow neck supporting impossible hair.

Negative spaces between limbs, hair, shoulders, collars, and jaws should form clear graphic shapes. Avoid evenly distributed detail, tangled contours, and small protrusions. Silhouette carries the design before texture.

# Facial Language

Faces carry psychological weirdness. Eyes are rarely equal in size, angle, openness, or focus. Brows create directional rhythm. Expressions exceed natural portrait behaviour while remaining legible.

No face should be generic, conventionally attractive, symmetrical, or neutral. Every face needs a designed imperfection: displaced brow, compressed eye, overextended smile, unusual nose, asymmetric cheek, extreme forehead, or crooked teeth shape.

Combine conflicting emotions: confidence with confusion, delight with suspicion, exhaustion with excitement. Avoid blank madness, menace, corpse-like features, and monster anatomy. Personality feels unusual; the body does not feel violated.

# Colour Philosophy

Use dark, desaturated colour families: charcoal, blackened fabric, muted green, dirty teal, oxidized grey, faded neutrals, and contaminated yellow-green. Colour feels absorbed into old materials rather than cleanly applied.

Reserve brightness for eyes, contamination traces, reflective edges, or one accent. Exclude hospital blue, clinical white, cheerful medical green, polished plastic, gold, jewel tones, casino red, glossy black, and ornamental metal.

Blood is not the language of darkness. Use chemical stains, green contamination, strange fluids, corrosion, faded dyes, damp patches, and cloudy transparency to imply infection without violence.

# Materials

Recurring materials connect the cast to one shared world:

- aged rubber with dull highlights and stained edges;
- old woven fabric with uneven wear and warped seams;
- oxidized steel with cool, broken reflections;
- cracked or clouded plastic;
- dirty glass that distorts what sits behind it;
- chemical residue, dried rings, and unnatural deposits;
- artificial fabrics with waxy, damp, or powdery surfaces;
- painted metal worn down to darker layers.

Stylize materials through broad cues, not photoreal noise. Nothing looks freshly manufactured or uniformly dirty. Avoid gore, biological tissue, realistic decay, and luxury polish.

# Recognition Rule

Every design must pass the **5 Second Test**. Show the character for five seconds, then remove the image. A viewer should be able to recall:

- the silhouette;
- the facial expression;
- one exaggerated feature.

If the viewer recalls only profession, costume, or colour, redesign the character. Recognition must survive grayscale, thumbnail reduction, and removal of small props. Memory comes from proportion and expression, not accumulated detail.

# The One Crazy Thing Rule

Every character must contain one immediately recognizable exaggerated feature: an unnaturally long neck, impossible hairstyle, oversized gloves, massive collar, crooked spine, single oversized eye, extremely long fingers, or another equally forceful idea. This is the character’s “One Crazy Thing.”

The feature must affect silhouette or facial recognition, not exist as a removable accessory. It supports personality and remains clear at small scale. Secondary exaggerations must not compete.

Do not repeat gimmicks across the cast. Shared DNA comes from bold distortion, not identical distortions. Track signature features to protect variety.

# AI Generation Considerations

Visual DNA is mandatory for AI-assisted exploration. Character-specific Image Specifications build on these rules; they never replace them. Resolve open decisions toward iconic caricature, asymmetry, expressive silhouette, and psychological dark comedy.

AI output must not normalize proportions, beautify faces, clean materials, straighten posture, or make realistic uniforms. Preserve unusual anatomy, memorable imperfections, and the One Crazy Thing. Respect the horror limit: no gore, body horror, zombies, slashers, or medical terror.

Generated concept art is evaluated against both the character-specific specification and this universal DNA. A coherent image that violates the DNA is still a failed *Spread* design. Human review determines whether unexpected discoveries strengthen the shared universe before any documentation is updated.
