# Image Specification Standard

## Purpose

An Image Specification is the compact visual source for generating concept art of one *Spread* symbol. It translates approved human design decisions into a structured description that an image-generation model can interpret without reading the full Character Bible.

Each symbol should have its own specification. The specification is used together with the shared [AI Generation Guide](ai-generation-guide.md).

## Length and Writing Style

Target approximately 150–300 words.

Write in direct, concrete visual language. Prefer short statements, explicit attributes, and observable relationships. State one coherent direction rather than presenting alternatives. Use consistent project terminology and organize information under stable headings.

Every character specification must actively define an iconic mascot identity. Prioritize unusual proportions, dominant facial features, expressive posture, memorable silhouette, and visual personality. Do not allow familiar professions or plausible anatomy to become the design concept.

Describe what must be visible. Do not explain the history of a decision or persuade the reader that it is correct.

## Required Content

An Image Specification should define:

- the subject’s visual role and hierarchy;
- dominant forms and identifying features;
- silhouette, caricature, and deliberately exaggerated proportion;
- distinctive face, expression, hair, and posture, when applicable;
- costume, objects, and visible materials;
- broad colour direction;
- framing, camera view, and background treatment;
- small-size readability priorities;
- prohibited visual interpretations;
- the intended immediate impression.

Specifications should favor iconic shapes over realistic anatomy, personality over professional accuracy, psychological weirdness over horror, and dark humour over violence. Unexpected choices must remain coherent enough to support a single readable mascot.

Every detail should contribute directly to the image. Include only approved decisions from the Character Bible or current art direction.

## Recommended Sections

### Role

One concise statement of visual hierarchy and function within the symbol family.

### Visual Identity

The defining mascot identity, unusual proportions, visual personality, shape language, and primary identifying features.

### Silhouette

The distinctive outer shape, posture, proportion rhythm, and features that must survive at thumbnail size.

### Face

Exaggerated facial proportions, dominant features, eyes, hair, expression, asymmetry, and psychological character. Omit when irrelevant.

### Costume

Clothing, equipment, accessories, materials, and their relative importance.

### Colours

Palette families, value relationships, accent placement, and colours to avoid. Exact values are optional unless approved.

### Composition

Crop, pose, viewing angle, focal area, canvas relationship, and background simplicity.

### Readability

The visual information that must remain clear when reduced to a slot-symbol icon.

### Avoid

A compact list of high-risk mistakes and prohibited interpretations specific to the subject.

### Target Impression

The immediate visual and emotional read expected from the finished concept.

## Content That Does Not Belong

Never include storytelling, scene prose, gameplay explanations, probabilities, payouts, technical implementation, engine terminology, design-meeting history, iteration logs, unresolved alternatives, generation syntax, model parameters, or long artistic essays.

Never default to ordinary believable humans, realistic professional uniforms, accurate anatomy, generic medical character design, or realism-led detail. Do not use blood, gore, zombies, body horror, slasher aesthetics, or hospital horror as shortcuts for darkness.

Do not copy the full Character Bible into this format. Distill it. If a detail cannot affect the visible result, it does not belong in the Image Specification.
